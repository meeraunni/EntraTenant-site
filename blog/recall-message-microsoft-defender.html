How to recall a message using Microsoft Defender

Outlook recall is not a control plane. Defender remediation is. The goal is to locate the message, scope blast radius, execute a removal action with auditability, and verify outcomes. This is incident response hygiene, not “try again and hope.”

2026-03-039 min readRemediationAIR
The remediation mindset: you are controlling the delivered artifact across mailboxes. Don’t start by deleting “a copy” in one inbox. Start by proving scope and then applying a controlled action across the tenant.

Architecture: where “recall” actually lives

Outlook recall is a client-side best-effort feature and fails in common realities: users on mobile, external recipients, different mail clients, message already read, message moved by rules, or protections changing the flow. Defender for Office 365 gives you a control plane: you query the message at rest and in transit, then remediate it using actions that the platform can apply across mailboxes.

Step 1: investigation and scoping in Explorer

Start in the Microsoft Defender portal and use Explorer (Threat Explorer) to locate the message. Your goal is not “find the subject line.” Your goal is to produce a defensible scope: which recipients received it, whether it was delivered, whether it was moved to junk/quarantine, whether there were clicks, and whether the payload is still present.

Use the strongest identifiers available. Message ID is the best. Subject + sender + time window is workable but less precise. Once you have the message record, confirm you’re working on the exact artifact by checking headers and delivery details.

Step 2: decide the remediation action based on risk

Defender actions are not equal. Choose the minimum action that achieves the safety objective. If the message is suspicious but you’re still validating, moving to Junk can be appropriate. If confirmed malicious, remove it. If you have downstream compromise indicators, remediation is just one part of the incident and should be paired with account/session actions.

When you execute a remediation action, record the reason, scope, and expected outcome. This is what you need later when someone asks why mail disappeared and whether it was handled consistently.

Step 3: AIR approvals and operational control

In tenants using Automated Investigation and Response (AIR), some actions may require approval. This can be intentional governance: avoid automatic hard deletes without human review. The engineering requirement is the same: the action must either execute or be approved and then execute. Don’t assume the click you made was the change. Verify the action status.

Step 4: verification (prove removal, not “looks ok”)

Verification means checking the action status in Defender, confirming the message no longer appears as delivered in Explorer, and spot-checking mailbox state for a subset of recipients. If the incident includes link clicks or credential entry, remediation must extend to identity: revoke sessions, reset credentials, and look for suspicious sign-ins.

Hardening: stop repeats with alert policies

Once you remediate, you should improve detection. Alert policies can give you early warning signals for patterns you care about. The operational goal is not to generate noise. It is to generate actionable alerts that correlate with your incident response playbook.
